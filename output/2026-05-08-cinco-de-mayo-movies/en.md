# The Cinco de Mayo Movies Worth Your Time (And the Ones That Aren't)

## 01. Why Most "Holiday Movie" Lists Get It Wrong

Every year around May 5th, streaming platforms and entertainment sites publish their lists of "best Cinco de Mayo movies." Most of these lists share the same problem: they conflate "films featuring Latino actors" with "films that actually say something about Mexican or Mexican-American experience." The distinction matters, and it's the lens I've used to put together what I actually think is worth watching.

Cinco de Mayo movies don't need to be celebratory in tone. They need to be honest.

## 02. The Films I'd Actually Recommend

There are a few films I've come back to across different years, and my opinion on each of them has only sharpened over time.

*Coco* (2017) is the obvious starting point, but it earns that position. Pixar's research into Día de los Muertos tradition goes beyond surface-level representation — the film genuinely understands that death in Mexican cultural memory isn't a rupture, it's a continuation. The emotional core of the film, a young boy caught between his family's prohibitions and his own artistic identity, isn't a manufactured conflict. It reflects something real. I'd recommend *Coco* as one of the best cinco de mayo movies not because it's festive, but because it's true.

*Frida* (2002) is a harder watch, and it's better for it. Salma Hayek spent a decade trying to get this film made, and the final product reflects that investment. The film borrows visual vocabulary from Kahlo's own paintings — surrealist, layered, unapologetically bodily — and uses it to tell a story about pain, politics, and identity that doesn't resolve neatly. If you haven't seen it, the Cinco de Mayo period is as good an occasion as any.

*Selena* (1997) deserves more critical attention than it typically receives. Jennifer Lopez's performance is the best of her career, and the film's central tension — a Mexican-American family navigating two cultures without fully belonging to either — is handled with more nuance than most biographical films manage. It's one of the cinco de mayo movies that stays with you in the way that only honest storytelling does.

## 03. The One Most Lists Skip

*A Million Miles Away* (2023) rarely appears on cinco de mayo movies lists, which is a mistake. The Amazon original follows José Hernández, a Mexican immigrant farmworker who eventually became a NASA astronaut. What makes it work as a film, not just as an inspirational story, is its refusal to smooth over the cost. The family's sacrifices are shown with specificity rather than sentiment. Michael Peña delivers a performance that carries the weight of the whole film.

It doesn't have the visual spectacle that makes *Coco* so instantly shareable, which is probably why it gets overlooked. But if you want a Cinco de Mayo film that engages directly with what Mexican-American life in the United States actually looks like — the labor, the aspiration, the negotiation of identity — this is the one I'd put on first.

## 05. On Watching With Intention

Cinco de Mayo as it's observed in the United States is largely a commercial construct, increasingly distant from how the holiday is actually understood in Mexico. That doesn't mean it's without value as a cultural moment — but it does mean the way you engage with it is worth thinking about.

Watching a film that takes Mexican or Mexican-American experience seriously is a more substantive form of engagement than most alternatives. The cinco de mayo movies I've listed here aren't a cultural obligation. They're good films that happen to offer a genuine point of entry into a history and a set of lived experiences that deserve more than one day's attention per year.

Two hours with *A Million Miles Away*, or an afternoon with *Coco* and *Selena* back-to-back — either way, you'll come away with something that holds.
